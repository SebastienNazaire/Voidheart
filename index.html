01 / Introduction

What Is Voidheart?

Voidheart is a tabletop roleplaying game of far-future spacefaring adventure, built on the bones of a beloved hope-and-fear dice system — reimagined for the stars. One player takes the role of the Navigator (the GM), painting the galaxy and all its dangers. The rest play Crew — explorers, soldiers, hackers, psionics, pilots, and drifters who push into the unknown together.

The game centers on a fundamental tension: Signal — connection, luck, ingenuity, and hope — versus Static — entropy, threat, chaos, and the cold indifference of the void. Every roll of the dice asks which force holds sway at this moment, in this corner of the galaxy.

"The void doesn't hate you. It doesn't even know you exist.
That's the part that should keep you up at night."

What You'll Need

Two twelve-sided dice in two different colors — designate one your Signal Die and one your Static Die. A handful of six-siders for damage. Character sheets. A Navigator screen (or nothing — play with your cards on the table). And people willing to stare into the dark together.

02 / Core Mechanics

The Signal & Static Roll

Whenever a Crew member attempts something risky, the Navigator calls for a roll. Grab your two d12s — Signal and Static — add the relevant Trait modifier, and compare them.

Reading the Roll

Result Outcome What Happens
BOTH show the same number Critical Signal Exceptional success. You gain 2 Signal tokens and describe something spectacular. The Navigator has no say in how it goes.
Signal > Static With Signal You succeed and gain 1 Signal token. If the roll is 17+, describe an added benefit.
Static > Signal With Static You succeed, but the Navigator gains 1 Static token and introduces a cost, complication, or threat.
Total < 10 Miss You don't succeed. The Navigator gains 1 Static token and narrates what goes wrong. The action still matters — the story moves.

Trait Scores

Characters have six traits. Add the relevant one to your total (both dice combined) when making a check.

Nerve — Combat, endurance, staying cool under fire.
Finesse — Stealth, piloting, precision work.
Edge — Hacking, science, technical problem-solving.
Presence — Persuasion, leadership, reading people.
Instinct — Sensing danger, survival, psionic attunement.
Mettle — Willpower, resisting harm, brute force.

Difficulty Thresholds

The Navigator sets a Difficulty before the roll: Standard (10), Tough (15), or Brutal (20). The total of both dice plus trait must meet or beat the threshold to succeed. The Signal vs. Static comparison still determines the tone of the success.

03 / Currencies

Signal & Static Tokens

Signal and Static are more than dice — they're the tidal forces of every scene. Both the Crew and the Navigator accumulate tokens and spend them to shape the story.

◈ Signal Tokens (Crew)

Earned on Signal rolls and Critical hits. Spend them to: push a failed roll into a success, add a detail to the world, heal 1 Vitality, resist an incoming consequence, or trigger a Vocation's Signal ability.

◇ Static Tokens (Navigator)

Earned on Static rolls and misses. Spend them to: introduce a new threat, trigger an adversary action outside normal order, make the environment more dangerous, or activate a named adversary's special move.

Token Limits

The Crew may hold up to 5 Signal tokens total, shared among the group. The Navigator may hold up to 6 Static tokens. Any earned beyond the cap is simply lost.

04 / Character Creation

Building Your Crew

Creating a Voidheart character takes four steps: choose a Lineage (your species and biological heritage), choose an Origin (where and how you grew up), pick a Vocation (your role aboard ship and in the galaxy), and set your Traits.

Step 1: Lineage

Lineage reflects your biology. Every lineage follows the same budget: 2 stat points, plus one passive. A threshold improvement is the passive for that lineage — those lineages don't get a separate ability on top of it.

TERRAN

Humanity spread from a blue world that most living Terrans have never seen.

Stats: Vit +1, Res +1
Adaptive: Once per session, re-roll any one die after seeing the result but before the consequence is narrated.

SYNTH

Artificial minds in synthetic bodies — some remember being made; others were born into it.

Stats: Vit +2
Environmental Immunity: Immune to Resolve damage from environmental sources — hard vacuum, toxins, extreme cold, the psychological weight of the void. Your mind doesn't register those as threats.

VEKHARI

Insectoid beings from a high-gravity hive world. Their colonies fell to the Rend three centuries ago.

Stats: Vit +2, Vit Minor threshold +1
The threshold is the passive. No separate ability.

LUMINAL

Energy-based entities in physical forms — "bodies" sustained by internal coherence fields.

Stats: Res +2
Phaseshift: Once per scene, pass through one solid obstacle up to 2m thick. You emerge on the other side intact.

GRAVBORN

Humans evolved or engineered for high-gravity worlds. Dense-boned, wide-framed, unstoppable.

Stats: Vit +2, Vit Major threshold +1
The threshold is the passive. No separate ability.

RIMFOLK

Born on ships, stations, or frontier rocks — raised between destinations, at home nowhere and everywhere.

Stats: Vit +1, Res +1
Shipwise: When aboard a ship, you may spend 1 Signal to treat any failed check as a With Static success instead — you get what you needed, but the Navigator still gains a Static token and introduces a complication.

Step 2: Origin

Origin is where you grew up and what it made you. Every origin follows the same budget: 3 stat points, where a threshold improvement counts as 1 point. Origins also grant a bonus skill, a starting contact, and gear.

HEGEMONY CORE

You grew up inside the Interstellar Hegemony — clean air, propaganda, and opportunity for those who don't ask too many questions.

Stats: Res +2, Res Minor threshold +1 — institutional conditioning breeds deep composure under pressure
Bonus Skill: Politics, Protocol
Contact: A Hegemony officer who still owes you something
Gear: Hegemony credentials (1 use before expiring)

FRONTIER COLONY

A small world at the edge of settled space. Self-sufficient and suspicious of authority by necessity.

Stats: Vit +3 — hard labour, hard weather, and the understanding that no one is coming to help you
Bonus Skill: Engineering, Survival
Contact: A colonist community that will harbour you, no questions asked
Gear: Frontier-issue multi-tool (never breaks)

WANDERING FLEET

You were born aboard one of the great migration fleets — a city of ships moving slow between distant stars.

Stats: Res +3 — years of close quarters, long hauls, and the relentless intimacy of shared purpose
Bonus Skill: Navigation, Trade
Contact: A fleet captain who'll give you passage anywhere on the route
Gear: Ancient star charts (accurate to a dead civilization)

STATION-BORN

Raised on a space station — corridors, hierarchies, and the politics of enclosed spaces were your whole world.

Stats: Vit +1, Res +2 — street-smart endurance that is neither purely physical nor purely mental
Bonus Skill: Streetwise, Black Market
Contact: A fixer in the station's underbelly who can move things quietly
Gear: Concealed holdout pistol, EMP charge ×2

RECLAIMED

You grew up in the ruins of something the Rend destroyed. Everything you have, you took back.

Stats: Vit +2, Vit Minor threshold +1 — a survivor's body is forged by necessity, not genetics
Bonus Skill: Combat, Scavenging
Contact: A cell of resistance fighters who trust you with their lives
Gear: Rend-pattern armour plating (worn, but functional)

DEEP SURVEY

Raised by scientists or explorers at the very edge of charted space. You've seen things most people haven't named yet.

Stats: Res +2, Res Major threshold +1 — exposure to the unknowable teaches you either to break or to become unbreakable
Bonus Skill: Xenology, Archaeology
Contact: A research institute that will trade information for information
Gear: Personal scanner with 3 unknown readings still queued

Step 3: Traits

Your six traits are Nerve, Finesse, Edge, Presence, Instinct, and Mettle. Rather than free point-buy, your Vocation gives you a fixed trait array — a set of six values you freely assign to the six traits in any order you like. Your Prime Trait is fixed by your Vocation; it's the trait your class abilities key off of, and it should generally receive your highest array value.

05 / Health & Stress

Vitality & Resolve

Characters track two harm tracks: Vitality (physical punishment your body can absorb) and Resolve (mental and emotional endurance). Both tracks are defined by three values: a total capacity (how many pips), a Minor Threshold, and a Major Threshold.

How Damage Works

When you take a hit, the Navigator says how much damage it deals. Compare that number to your thresholds — not your current pips — to determine how many pips you mark.

Below Minor
Mark 1 pip. Glancing blow, superficial wound, passing fear.
At or above Minor
Mark 2 pips. Real harm — you'll feel this later.
At or above Major
Mark 3 pips + a Condition. Serious injury or psychological break.

When all Vitality pips are filled: Incapacitated (unconscious or dying — another character must stabilize you before you can act). When all Resolve pips are filled: Broken (you are overwhelmed — you cannot act offensively until you receive aid or rest).

Thresholds by Vocation

Every Vocation sets your base Vitality and Resolve values. A Fleet Warden is a wall of physical endurance with modest mental defense; a Void Seeker is fragile in a firefight but nearly unshakeable in the mind. Lineage modifiers apply on top of these base values.

Armor & Shielding

Armor reduces incoming physical damage by its value before you compare to thresholds. Shielding works the same for Resolve damage. Both refresh at the start of each new encounter. A hit reduced below 1 by Armor still counts as a hit — mark 1 pip regardless.

Recovery

Short Rest (10 min): Spend 1 Signal token to clear 2 Vitality pips or 2 Resolve pips. A Savant or Fleet Warden crewmate may assist, allowing you to clear pips without spending Signal once per short rest.

Long Rest (8 hrs, safe location): Clear all pips. Conditions require a successful Mettle or Edge roll (Difficulty 10) or appropriate treatment to clear — they don't go away on their own.

06 / Vocations & Domains

Vocations & Domains

Your Vocation is your character class — your identity and role aboard ship. There are eight Vocations. Each one has fixed Vitality/Resolve stats (see Section 05), a Prime Trait, a unique Signal Ability, and access to two Domains.

Domains are the ability pools you draw Maneuvers from. They are not Vocations — they're thematic libraries of moves. At creation, pick three Maneuvers total from your Vocation's two Domains. As you advance, you pick more.

The Eight Vocations

Each Vocation lists its two Domains and Signal Ability. The Prime Trait for each is set in the trait array table in Step 3 of character creation. No two Vocations share the same domain pair.

FLEET WARDEN

IRON · TIDE

Niche: frontline combatant, crew protector, last line of defence.

You are the wall between your crew and the galaxy's teeth. You fight, you endure, and you lead from the front. The Tide domain isn't about commanding from a bridge — it's about inspiring people while standing next to them.

Signal Ability — Rally: Spend 1 Signal. One crewmate takes an immediate free action outside initiative order and clears 1 Vitality pip.

COMMANDER

TIDE · VECTOR

Niche: tactical coordinator, fleet engagement, strategic resource management.

You lead through clarity, foresight, and an understanding of space that makes the crew feel like they're always in the right place. You're not the one flying the ship — you're the one who knows where it needs to be three moves from now.

Signal Ability — Tactical Override: Spend 2 Signal. Redirect any one adversary action — after it's announced but before it resolves — to a different target of your choice.

DRIFTER PILOT

VECTOR · SABLE

Niche: ship handling, evasion, getting in and out of places you weren't invited.

You fly by feel and spite. The Sable domain gives you the underworld contacts and improvised audacity that turns a flight plan into something more interesting — landing in places that aren't on charts, with cargo that isn't on manifests.

Signal Ability — Emergency Burn: Spend 1 Signal. Automatically succeed on any piloting check — no roll — once per scene. The ship takes 1d6 Hull damage from drive stress.

GHOST OPERATIVE

SHADOW · GRID

Niche: infiltration, electronic intrusion, assassination, leaving no trace.

You move through physical and digital spaces with equal ease. Where the Savant brute-forces systems from the outside, you're already inside the building when the hack completes. The combination of Shadow and Grid makes you a professional absence.

Signal Ability — Clean Slate: Spend 2 Signal. Completely erase your presence from one electronic system, one set of surveillance records, or one verified witness's memory.

CORSAIR

SABLE · IRON

Niche: scrappy combat, underworld dealing, improvised solutions under pressure.

You operate in the space between laws. Unlike the Ghost Operative, who avoids confrontation, you lean into it — fights, deals, heists. The Iron domain means you can handle yourself when things go wrong, which they do regularly.

Signal Ability — Always an Angle: Spend 1 Signal. Declare a resource, contact, or escape route you "had prepared." The Navigator must make it true, or spend 2 Static to complicate it instead.

SAVANT

GRID · CIPHER

Niche: system mastery, field medicine, engineering, scientific analysis.

You understand systems — digital, mechanical, biological. Where the Ghost Operative uses Grid for access, you use it for control. Cipher is your other half: you build things, fix things, and understand why things are broken in the first place.

Signal Ability — System Crack: Spend 1 Signal. Gain complete control of one electronic system — weapons, doors, comms, life support — for one scene. No roll required.

VOID SEEKER

DEEP · PSIONIC

Niche: xenoarchaeology, ancient tech, psionic sensing, alien communication.

Something out in the dark has been calling to you. You've already heard it. Where the Empath reads living minds, you read dead civilizations — ruins, artifacts, the psychic residue of species that no longer exist. The Psionic domain amplifies what you find there.

Signal Ability — Resonance: Spend 1 Signal. Ask the Navigator one truthful question about any ruin, artifact, alien presence, or ancient technology in the current scene. They must answer honestly.

EMPATH

PSIONIC · SHADOW

Niche: social manipulation, psionic projection, reading and moving through people.

You feel what others feel — sometimes before they do. Unlike the Void Seeker, who turns psionics outward toward the ancient and alien, you turn them inward toward living minds. The Shadow domain isn't about hiding from people — it's about moving through them.

Signal Ability — Projection: Spend 2 Signal. Project an emotion, sensation, or false memory into one target's mind. They act on it as real until end of scene or until something breaks the effect.

07 / Starships

Ships: The Sixth Crew Member

Your ship has its own stat block, its own harm tracks, and it changes over time — upgraded, damaged, repaired, and named. No two ships play the same way. At the start of a campaign, the crew picks a Ship Class, which sets all base stats. Everything beyond that is earned.

Ship Stats Explained

Hull Points (HP): Total damage the ship can absorb. When Hull Points hit 0, the ship is destroyed (or breaking apart — the Navigator decides).
Armor: Reduces all incoming physical damage before it touches Hull Points. Applied before threshold comparison.
Minor / Major Threshold: Determines how bad each hit is — like character Vitality thresholds. Compare the damage after Armor to these values to find out how many Breach slots you mark.
Breach Slots: Named conditions that activate when marked. Each class has a fixed list. Marking all Breach slots = Critical Failure (ship cannot maneuver or fire).
Systems: Electronic integrity — depleted by hacking, EMP, and sensor attacks. Systems have their own Minor/Major thresholds and their own condition list.
Thrust: Modifier added to all Pilot rolls for maneuvering and evasion.
Armament: Damage dice rolled when the ship's weapons fire successfully.

How Ship Damage Works

When a weapon hits your ship, subtract Armor from the damage, then compare the result to your thresholds:

Below Minor
Hull Points reduced by the damage amount. No Breach marked. Hull is taking a beating but systems hold.
At or above Minor
Hull Points reduced + mark 1 Breach slot. Something breaks.
At or above Major
Hull Points reduced + mark 2 Breach slots. Serious structural damage.

When a Breach slot is marked, the Navigator picks which condition activates — or uses the ship's pre-listed conditions in order. Each condition imposes an immediate ongoing penalty. When HP hits 0, the ship is gone regardless of remaining Breach slots.

Universal Breach Conditions

Each ship class has a selection from this list. When a breach is marked, that condition is immediately active until repaired.

  • Thrusters Compromised: Thrust −2. Stacks if marked again.
  • Weapon Systems Offline: The ship cannot fire until this breach is repaired.
  • Life Support Critical: Each round of combat, all crew take 1 Resolve damage from the alarms, pressure drop, and panic.
  • Sensor Blackout: The crew fires blind. All attack rolls made at disadvantage.
  • Jump Drive Locked: Hyperspace entry is impossible until repaired. You're not going anywhere fast.
  • Hull Integrity Compromised: All future hits deal +3 damage (before Armor) due to stress fractures.
  • Reactor Unstable: The next hit that marks a breach triggers an internal explosion — all crew take 2d6 Vitality damage, no Armor applies.

Repairing Breaches

Repairing a breach requires an Edge roll (Difficulty 12 for most breaches, 16 for Reactor Unstable or Jump Drive Locked) and at least 10 minutes of work. A Savant character reduces all repair Difficulties by 3. Hull Points do not recover automatically — restoring HP requires a starport, a supply run, or extended downtime.

Ship Actions in Combat

Each round, the Pilot chooses one Ship Action on their turn, separate from their personal action:

  • Attack Vector — Roll Finesse + Thrust vs. Difficulty set by enemy Thrust. On success, the ship's Armament fires.
  • Evasive Pattern — Roll Finesse + Thrust (Difficulty 12). Success: impose +3 Difficulty on the next attack targeting you this round.
  • Emergency Jump — Attempt a hasty hyperspace entry. Roll Instinct (Difficulty 15). On success: escaped. On failure: you jump somewhere wrong — Navigator decides where.
  • Full Burn — Thrust becomes +6 this round. Next round, Thrust drops to −2 while drives vent heat. Can't be used two rounds in a row.

Upgrades

Between sessions, at a starport or with the right parts, the crew can upgrade their ship. Each upgrade costs either credits or a significant favor. Suggested upgrades include:

Reinforced Hull: +5 Hull Points, Minor threshold +1
Overdrive Thrusters: Thrust +2 permanently
Weapons Upgrade: Armament increases one die size (d6 → d8 → d10)
ECM Suite: Systems +4, enemies suffer −2 on all scan rolls
Emergency Gel Foam: Once per session, automatically negate one Breach condition
Black Box: If the ship is destroyed, crew survives in emergency pods — no guaranteed destination

09 / Navigator's Guide

Running the Stars

As the Navigator, you don't play to win or to challenge the Crew into failure. You play to find out what happens when interesting people collide with impossible situations. The galaxy exists to be explored — and to push back.

Navigator Principles

01

Portray a galaxy that lives without the Crew. Factions make moves. Politics shift. Ships depart. The universe doesn't pause while the characters are sleeping. Events should feel like consequences, not coincidences.

02

Spend Static loudly. When you spend a Static token, name what you're doing and why. "I'm spending 2 Static — the hull scanner finds an incoming signal. It's encrypted. It's not from anywhere charted." Static should feel purposeful, not punishing.

03

Make the void feel vast. Distance is time. Time is cost. Every jump is a commitment. When the Crew arrives somewhere, make them feel the strangeness of having crossed light-years. Sounds, smells, gravity. Alien skies.

04

Misses move the story forward. A miss is never a dead end — it's a complication. If the crew fails to hack the door, the door is still there, but so is the security team that got the alert.

05

The Rend is real, and it's still out there. Whatever destroyed the Vekhari homeworld three centuries ago didn't stop. It got quiet. Work that into the background like static in a radio signal — always present, never fully resolved.

06

Ask questions and use the answers. Where did your character get that scar? What's the ship's name and why? Who left that photo in the locker? Their answers are your best material.

Scene Clocks

Use Clocks to track ongoing threats: a 4-segment clock fills as Static is spent or missed rolls occur. When a clock fills, something bad happens — reinforcements arrive, the reactor overloads, the negotiations collapse. Show clocks to the players. Tension should be visible.

Factions

The galaxy has three dominant powers and several wild cards. Each has an Agenda, an Asset, and a Pressure — what they want, what they'll trade, and how they squeeze.

THE INTERSTELLAR HEGEMONY

Agenda: Maintain economic and political control over the Core systems. Asset: Unlimited naval resources and legitimate authority. Pressure: Warrants, blockades, and economic sanctions. They rarely get their hands dirty — they don't need to.

THE FREE DRIFT COMPACT

Agenda: Protect the autonomy of independent stations and wandering fleets. Asset: Information networks, safe harbors, and mutual aid. Pressure: Trade embargoes and whisper campaigns — they win through information and solidarity, not firepower.

THE DEEP ARCHIVE

Agenda: Recover and control xenotech from dead civilizations — and prevent the next Rend event. Asset: Ancient technology that shouldn't exist and knowledge no one else has. Pressure: They know secrets. Sometimes they share them. Mostly they don't.

10 / Quick Reference

The Core Loop

  1. Navigator describes a situation and invites action.
  2. Crew declares intent and approach.
  3. Navigator determines Trait and Difficulty (10 / 15 / 20).
  4. Roll Signal + Static + Trait. Signal higher = succeed + gain Signal. Static higher = succeed + Navigator gains Static. Total misses = fail + Navigator gains Static.
  5. Describe the result. Adjust tokens. Advance the scene.
